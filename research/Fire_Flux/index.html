Impact of Fire on Carbon Flux in North-Western Himalaya

July 15, 2025

Forests play a crucial role as major carbon reservoirs in terrestrial ecosystems, primarily acting as carbon sinks by taking in atmospheric carbon through photosynthesis. However, during extreme disturbances such as forest fires, they shift from being carbon sinks to becoming carbon sources. Ongoing climate change has significantly altered the stability of forest ecosystems, increasing their susceptibility to extreme disturbances. Among these disturbances, forest fires have become a major contributor to carbon loss, especially in fire-prone areas such as the North-Western Himalaya (NWH). This research examined variations in carbon flux caused by fire events across this ecologically fragile region using the Functionally Assembled Terrestrial Ecosystem Simulator (FATES) integrated within the Community Land Model Version 5 (CLM 5.0.3), which is a process-based Dynamic Global Vegetation Model (DGVM). The simulations were conducted for the year 2016 to analyze monthly patterns of fire occurrence, carbon emissions, and forest productivity, with particular emphasis on Gross Primary Productivity (GPP).

The model successfully represented the seasonal pattern of GPP, showing reduced values in winter months (January–February), followed by a steady rise during spring, reaching a peak in late summer (August–September), and then declining towards the end of the year. A comparison with eddy covariance (EC) observations from flux tower locations at Barkot and Haldwani showed strong agreement, with R² values exceeding 0.7, indicating that the model effectively captured ecosystem-level productivity. Despite this, the simulation results showed no fire occurrences during the study period, with the burned area recorded as zero. This inconsistency is likely due to the use of coarse spatial resolution, the dominance of small-scale fire events in the region, and the limited representation of human-induced fires in the model setup.

Observations indicate that small but frequent fire incidents occur during the summer months, particularly in April and May, leading to notable carbon emissions. These emissions were mainly concentrated in the southeastern part of the study region.

The findings highlight the intricate relationship between fire activity, human influence, and ecosystem productivity in the NWH. The study demonstrates the effectiveness of CLM5-FATES modeling frameworks in capturing seasonal ecosystem dynamics, while also pointing out their limitations in representing disturbance-related carbon fluxes, especially when anthropogenic influences are underrepresented. Further improvements in the model, including the integration of high-resolution fire datasets and better representation of human-driven fire processes, are necessary for more accurate assessment of forest carbon dynamics in densely populated and disturbance-prone regions.
