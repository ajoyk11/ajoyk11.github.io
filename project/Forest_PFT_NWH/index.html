Forest PFT Classification using Remote Sensing & Machine Learning

Ajoy Karmakar
June- July, 2024 

The concept of Plant Functional Types (PFTs) provides a robust framework for grouping vegetation based on shared structural, phenological, and physiological characteristics, as well as their functional responses to environmental conditions. Unlike biome-level classifications, which represent broad vegetation zones such as deserts, savannahs, and deciduous forests, PFTs enable a finer and more process-oriented representation of ecosystem functioning. This becomes particularly important in ecosystem modeling, where species-level parameterization is often constrained by the lack of detailed trait information.

In the present study, an advanced methodology was developed to classify forest PFTs in the North-Western Himalayan (NWH) region using Fourier Transformation of time-series NDVI data coupled with a Random Forest machine learning algorithm. The Fourier decomposition allowed extraction of key temporal features such as amplitude, phase, and mean vegetation dynamics, which are critical indicators of vegetation phenology and functional behavior.

The extracted Fourier features were used as inputs to the Random Forest classifier, enabling the differentiation of vegetation types based on their temporal signatures rather than solely on spectral characteristics. The classification results demonstrated strong performance, with an overall accuracy of 0.8257 and a Cohen’s Kappa coefficient of 0.7943, indicating substantial agreement with ground truth observations.

In contrast, the MODIS PFT product (MOD12Q1) showed significantly lower performance in representing the actual vegetation distribution of the region. The product yielded an overall accuracy of 0.3329 and a Cohen’s Kappa of 0.2126, indicating poor agreement with field observations. Notably, in regions such as the Shiwalik range, Dehradun, and Rishikesh, areas dominated by deciduous forests were incorrectly classified as evergreen forests, highlighting limitations in the global classification scheme when applied to complex mountainous terrains.

The discrepancies observed suggest that global PFT products may not adequately capture regional heterogeneity, particularly in ecologically sensitive and topographically diverse areas like the NWH. The proposed methodology demonstrates that integrating time-series analysis with machine learning provides a more reliable and region-specific classification framework.

Furthermore, the study indicates that a single-level Fourier decomposition captures significant phenological variation across vegetation types. However, areas with low and stable NDVI signals remain challenging to classify, suggesting that incorporating higher-order harmonics or additional temporal features could further improve classification accuracy.

Overall, the results emphasize the need for regionally calibrated PFT datasets for improved ecosystem modeling. The integration of ground truth data with machine learning algorithms such as Random Forest proves to be a powerful approach for generating accurate and scalable PFT maps, which are essential for understanding ecosystem sensitivity and carbon dynamics under changing climatic conditions.
